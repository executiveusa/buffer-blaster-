Beauty brands spend on Instagram and TikTok because that's where the engagement is. Pinterest gets treated as an afterthought — a place to dump product shots and forget.

That's a mistake, and the reason is arithmetic.

## The half-life problem

A TikTok has about 48 hours to earn its impressions. An Instagram Reel, maybe a week. After that, the algorithm has moved on and the content is effectively dead.

A Pinterest pin keeps getting clicks for **months**. We have pins from a year ago still sending traffic to a client's product pages. No other platform does that.

## The intent gap

People on TikTok are killing time. People on Pinterest are planning a purchase — a wedding look, a skincare routine, a kitchen renovation. The intent to buy is built into the session.

For a beauty brand, that means a Pinterest viewer is further along the funnel than a TikTok viewer. You're not interrupting them. You're answering a search they already made.

## What actually works

- **Vertical, 2:3 ratio.** Square pins underperform. Always vertical.
- **Text overlay on the image.** Pinterest is a visual search engine — the words on the pin are how people find it.
- **One product, one claim.** Don't make a collage. A single serum bottle with "fades dark spots in 4 weeks" outperforms a grid of eight products.
- **Pin consistently, not in bursts.** The algorithm rewards steady output. Two pins a day beats fourteen pins on Sunday.

## The honest caveat

Pinterest is slow. You won't see results in the first month. You'll see them in month four, when the pins you made in month one are still circulating. Most brands quit before that happens.

That's the opportunity. The brands that stay get a traffic source that compounds while everyone else is refreshing their TikTok analytics.
