The For You algorithm doesn't care about your editing. It cares whether the viewer is still watching at the three-second mark. Everything after that is a reward for getting the first two seconds right.

We track hook performance across every client. Five patterns keep showing up in our top performers. None of them are clever. All of them work.

## 1. The specific claim

> *We roasted 1,000 beans and only 12 made the cut.*

Specificity stops the scroll because it sounds like a real story, not a tagline. "We make great coffee" is invisible. "1,000 beans, 12 made it" makes you want to know which twelve.

## 2. The unexpected truth

> *The ingredient dermatologists won't name on camera.*

This works because it implies there's something the viewer doesn't know — and the video is about to tell them. Use it honestly. If the payoff doesn't deliver, the comment section will punish you.

## 3. The before/after frame

> *She used our serum for 30 days. Here's day 14.*

The structure does the work. The viewer commits because they want to see day 30. This is why transformation content converts — the hook is the promise of completion.

## 4. The sensory open (food only)

> *Listen to this.* [sound of crust cracking]

For food brands, sound is a cheat code. A two-second audio cue before any text appears hijacks the viewer's curiosity before their brain decides to scroll.

## 5. The insider reveal

> *Three things nobody tells you about [your category].*

List hooks survive because they set an expectation of completeness. The viewer feels they're missing something. Three is the magic number — short enough to finish, long enough to feel substantive.

## The rule underneath all of them

Every hook here creates an information gap. The viewer knows something is coming, and they have to keep watching to get it. If your first two seconds don't open a gap, no amount of editing will save the watch time.

Write the hook first. Shoot the video to serve it. That order matters.
